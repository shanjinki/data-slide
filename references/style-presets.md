# Style Presets

Generated reports should look like finished business material, not an exported notebook. Pick a coherent design direction before generating the final report, then keep all sections inside that system.

## Preview Policy

Default: infer one style and generate the final report.

Use 3 previews when:

- the report is for leadership or external-facing discussion
- the user has already complained about ugly AI outputs
- the data has no obvious visual direction
- the user explicitly wants options

Preview mix:

1. a safe executive style
2. a domain-matched style
3. a more distinctive wildcard style

Never render internal labels like "preview", "template", or "style option" inside the report page itself. Those names belong in the agent's message, not the artifact.

## command-center

Best for: operations, project governance, demand pools, risk reviews, incident reports, executive control-room summaries.

- Background: deep navy / ink black.
- Accent: cyan, indigo, emerald, amber, red.
- Typography: high-contrast sans-serif; compact but readable.
- Components: KPI cards, glowing borders, lifecycle funnels, risk tags, category grids, drilldown blocks.
- Narrative tone: decisive, analytical, urgent where needed.
- Avoid for: reports intended for print-heavy board memos.

## boardroom-light

Best for: management summaries, finance, CRM, consulting-style reviews, cross-department alignment.

- Background: warm white or very light gray.
- Accent: navy, slate, green, amber.
- Typography: calm sans-serif, strong hierarchy, restrained numerals.
- Components: executive memo, KPI tiles, ranked tables, clean bar charts, assumption panels.
- Narrative tone: restrained, authoritative, readable in print.
- Avoid for: highly visual consumer/customer insight reports that need energy.

## retail-pulse

Best for: ecommerce orders, reviews, campaigns, customer feedback, store/platform comparisons.

- Background: soft light canvas with saturated accents.
- Accent: coral, teal, blue, violet, yellow.
- Typography: friendly but sharp.
- Components: sales cards, trend strips, product grids, opportunity/risk badges, review snippets.
- Narrative tone: commercial, energetic, action-oriented.
- Avoid for: strict compliance or audit reports.

## ops-ledger

Best for: ERP, sales order fulfillment, receivables, inventory, procurement, warehouse, supply chain, process quality, exception management.

- Background: charcoal or paper-like ledger.
- Accent: green, amber, steel blue.
- Typography: tabular, precise, operations-friendly.
- Components: exception lists, stock risk badges, warehouse/category blocks, heat strips, movement summaries.
- Narrative tone: precise, operational, reliable.
- Avoid for: marketing or customer-facing decks.

## editorial-brief

Best for: survey results, research synthesis, customer interviews, offline collection forms, qualitative + quantitative summaries.

- Background: paper, off-white, or muted editorial canvas.
- Accent: ink, sage, muted red, warm yellow.
- Typography: editorial heading + readable body.
- Components: finding cards, quote/excerpt blocks, segment comparisons, recommendation memo.
- Narrative tone: thoughtful, human, insight-led.
- Avoid for: dense operational dashboards where speed scanning matters more than interpretation.

## data-studio

Best for: generic data exploration, mixed business tables, analytics-first reports, unknown schemas.

- Background: neutral dark or light studio.
- Accent: blue, teal, amber, violet.
- Typography: clean analytical hierarchy.
- Components: dataset profile, distribution cards, numeric summaries, missingness panels, suggested analyses.
- Narrative tone: exploratory, transparent, methodical.
- Avoid for: final executive reports when a stronger domain style is clear.

## ink-wash

Best for: strategy memos, research synthesis, customer interviews, qualitative-plus-quantitative reports.

- Background: warm paper with subtle vertical grain.
- Accent: ink red, charcoal, muted earth tones.
- Typography: serif-like headline energy with readable business body text.
- Components: memo blocks, insight cards, ranked excerpts, restrained KPI tiles.
- Narrative tone: reflective, precise, senior.
- Avoid for: dense operational dashboards that need fast exception scanning.

## terminal-green

Best for: technical operations, data audits, incident reviews, log-like exports, engineering-facing governance.

- Background: near-black terminal canvas.
- Accent: green, lime, low-saturation status colors.
- Typography: monospaced feeling for numeric and table-heavy material.
- Components: command-style KPI panels, compact tables, audit rows, exception traces.
- Narrative tone: terse, factual, diagnostic.
- Avoid for: polished external leadership memos or print-heavy finance reports.

## neon-noir

Best for: consumer trends, competitive scans, campaign reviews, product launches, reports that need visual punch.

- Background: black-violet stage.
- Accent: cyan and magenta.
- Typography: sharp sans-serif, high-contrast numbers.
- Components: luminous KPI tiles, trend blocks, opportunity/risk cards, bold category comparisons.
- Narrative tone: energetic, directional, market-aware.
- Avoid for: conservative finance, compliance, or audit contexts.

## swiss-grid

Best for: consulting-style executive pages, KPI briefs, boardroom summaries, clean one-page decision reports.

- Background: white with strict grid texture.
- Accent: red and blue.
- Typography: disciplined sans-serif, strong alignment, compact labels.
- Components: square cards, ranked bars, executive grids, clear assumptions.
- Narrative tone: crisp, structured, no-nonsense.
- Avoid for: reports that need warm storytelling or rich qualitative excerpts.

## blueprint

Best for: process design, delivery planning, project reviews, operations architecture, workflow diagnosis.

- Background: deep blue blueprint field.
- Accent: pale blue linework.
- Typography: structured, technical, diagram-friendly.
- Components: lifecycle blocks, funnel stages, plan/risk cards, process grids.
- Narrative tone: systematic, explanatory, build-oriented.
- Avoid for: customer-facing or emotionally expressive reports.

## copper-report

Best for: finance, procurement, manufacturing, supplier reviews, cost and margin discussions.

- Background: warm ledger canvas.
- Accent: copper and amber.
- Typography: solid business hierarchy, numerals foregrounded.
- Components: variance cards, supplier/entity comparisons, cost blocks, exception tables.
- Narrative tone: grounded, commercial, disciplined.
- Avoid for: youth-market, campaign, or bright consumer reports.

## editorial-red

Best for: board memos, issue briefs, risk narratives, public-affairs style summaries.

- Background: warm editorial white.
- Accent: strong red and black.
- Typography: newspaper-like headline weight with clean body text.
- Components: headline insights, redline risks, compact charts, priority callouts.
- Narrative tone: assertive, editorial, high-signal.
- Avoid for: neutral data profiling where the story is not yet clear.

## glass-board

Best for: modern leadership dashboards, cross-team business reviews, product/ops mixed reports.

- Background: dark glass canvas.
- Accent: teal and cool blue.
- Typography: polished sans-serif with airy spacing.
- Components: translucent cards, KPI rows, comparison panels, action cards.
- Narrative tone: modern, calm, executive.
- Avoid for: reports that must print cleanly in black and white.

## midnight-gold

Best for: executive scorecards, investor-style summaries, annual/quarterly reviews, ceremonial briefings.

- Background: near-black with gold accents.
- Accent: gold and warm neutrals.
- Typography: premium headline feeling, restrained body.
- Components: scorecards, ranked performance blocks, risk panels, leadership summary.
- Narrative tone: formal, premium, concise.
- Avoid for: routine team standups or highly operational exception queues.

## paper-ledger

Best for: reconciliation, receivables, expense audits, offline collection sheets, finance operations.

- Background: pale ledger paper with ruled texture.
- Accent: green and yellow.
- Typography: readable, document-like, numerically stable.
- Components: ledger cards, aging buckets, exception tables, reconciliation notes.
- Narrative tone: careful, accountable, audit-friendly.
- Avoid for: highly visual marketing or consumer trend reports.

## cobalt-grid

Best for: sales operations, pipeline war rooms, performance tracking, channel management.

- Background: cobalt grid.
- Accent: yellow and cyan.
- Typography: confident, energetic, KPI-forward.
- Components: revenue cards, owner rankings, stage blocks, target gaps.
- Narrative tone: competitive, direct, action-oriented.
- Avoid for: subdued board memos or sensitive HR material.

## highlighter

Best for: workshops, action plans, lightweight decision notes, manager-facing follow-ups.

- Background: off-white with marker-like bands.
- Accent: black, yellow, green.
- Typography: direct and readable.
- Components: annotated KPI cards, action lists, short risks, highlight strips.
- Narrative tone: practical, direct, facilitation-friendly.
- Avoid for: formal external reports or heavily branded executive material.

## Selection Rules

- Leadership + risk: `command-center` or `boardroom-light`.
- Ecommerce/customer: `retail-pulse`.
- ERP/order-to-cash/supply/warehouse/process: `ops-ledger`.
- Survey/research/feedback: `editorial-brief`.
- Technical audit/incident: `terminal-green` or `blueprint`.
- Consulting one-pager: `swiss-grid`.
- Finance/procurement with stronger character: `copper-report`, `paper-ledger`, or `midnight-gold`.
- Distinctive consumer/market narrative: `neon-noir` or `editorial-red`.
- Workshop/action-planning output: `highlighter`.
- Unknown/mixed table: `data-studio` for profiling, then switch once the domain is clearer.
- If the generated report looks generic, revise the visual system before revising small details.

## Design Quality Rules

- Use a real report composition: hero, KPI row, insight block, analytical modules, action section.
- Use charts or chart-like visuals with clear labels and business meaning.
- Keep the visual grammar consistent across cards, charts, tables, badges, and recommendations.
- Use color for meaning, not decoration only.
- Avoid purple-gradient-on-white defaults, random card piles, and unstyled dataframes.
- Keep generated HTML self-contained; do not depend on external chart libraries.
